About

The person behind the parts.

Mechanical engineering student, Virginia Tech. I like making things — whether that's a moped that actually runs, a race car fixture, or a miniature statue for my parents' shelf.

Charlie Steinman — Blacksburg, VA

I like building things that didn't exist before I started.

I'm a mechanical engineering student at Virginia Tech finishing an accelerated master's program with a focus on metal additive manufacturing — specifically laser powder bed fusion and directed energy deposition. Day-to-day that means a lot of time in NX and Creo running structural simulations, but it started much earlier: tearing apart a dead lawn mower to reverse-engineer the piston, designing a model engine in 8th grade just to see if I could.

In the summers between semesters I've had the chance to do that for real — nine concrete counterweight concepts at Caterpillar for a new Small Wheel Loader, and component design for four Hendrick Motorsports NASCAR Cup Series cars (5, 9, 24, and 48). Both internships pushed me to work within real constraints — tolerances, schedules, supply chains — rather than the relatively clean sandbox of class projects.

Outside of coursework I'm a chassis engineer on Virginia Tech's BOLT electric motorcycle team, a teaching assistant for sophomore thermodynamics, and a competitive swimmer. On weekends I'm usually working on something that has nothing to do with my thesis: moped engine mods, an RC plane I designed from scratch, or an ESP32 project that started as a gift and turned into a whole electronics rabbit hole.

Long-term I'm interested in how additive manufacturing changes what's actually possible to build — topology-optimized structures, novel alloy combinations, geometry that couldn't be cast or machined. I want to work at the edge of what's manufacturable, wherever that happens to be.
