Satellite Integration & Test

Integration & Test Mechanical Engineering Cleanroom (ISO 5 / 100K) Cygnus ISS Resupply Satellite Assembly TVAC Testing Dulles, VA Building 5

The Environment

Northrop Grumman's Building 5 in Dulles, VA is a large-scale spacecraft integration facility. Most of my time was spent inside ISO Class 8 (100,000-count) cleanrooms — gowned up in bunny suits, on my feet for most of the shift, working directly on flight hardware alongside technicians and senior engineers.

The stakes are different from anything in a machine shop or on a racetrack. Every fastener, every lift, every piece of handling hardware matters because the hardware it's attached to will never come back for a warranty repair.

Cygnus ISS Resupply Spacecraft

The primary program I worked on was Cygnus — Northrop Grumman's uncrewed cargo spacecraft that delivers supplies, equipment, and experiments to the International Space Station under NASA's Commercial Resupply Services contract. Each vehicle is assembled, integrated, and tested at the Dulles facility before shipping to the launch site.

My role sat within the Integration and Testing mechanical engineering department, supporting the hands-on assembly side of the program. Even as a second-year intern, I've been cleared to work directly on flight hardware — helping dress and undress Cygnus in its MLI (multi-layer insulation) blanketing, moving it into and out of thermal vacuum chamber (TVAC) testing, assisting with component lifts using overhead cranes and lifting fixtures, and plenty of hands-on wrenching alongside the technician team.

Key Contributions

  • Torque star-pattern system — developed a bolt torque sequencing pattern to simplify and standardize the mating of Cygnus's Service Module to the Pressurized Cargo Module, reducing setup complexity for the technician team.
  • Lifting fixture proof load audit — recalculated and streamlined the proof load specifications for over 200 satellite lifting fixtures, applying structural analysis principles to ensure safety and compliance across mission-critical hardware operations.
  • Acoustic test instrumentation — installed over 50 accelerometers across the spacecraft ahead of acoustic testing, which simulates the frequencies and vibration environment of an actual launch.

What It Taught Me

Working in a cleanroom on flight hardware is a fundamentally different kind of discipline from design work. The design phase is over — now every decision is about not breaking what already exists. That constraint sharpens your attention to handling procedures, torque specs, and sequencing in a way that's hard to replicate anywhere else.

It also gave me a ground-level view of how a large aerospace integration program actually moves: the coordination between the ME department, technicians, quality, and program management; the role of travelers and work orders; and what it looks like when a team of specialists assembles something that has to work perfectly, once, in orbit.
